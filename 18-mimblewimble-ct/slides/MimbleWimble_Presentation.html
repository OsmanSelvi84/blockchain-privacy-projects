01 · The problem

Public blockchains expose every amount.

On Bitcoin, the ledger is fully transparent: anyone can read who paid whom and exactly how much. For real payments — salaries, business flows — that is unacceptable. The challenge: hide the amounts, but keep the chain publicly verifiable so no one can cheat.

02 · The privacy landscape

Three ways to add privacy.

Monero

Ring signatures

Hides sender, receiver, and amount with ring signatures, stealth addresses, and RingCT. Larger transactions.

Zcash

zk-SNARKs

Hides everything with zero-knowledge proofs — powerful, but historically needed a trusted setup.

MimbleWimble

Commitments

Hides amounts with homomorphic Pedersen commitments. No addresses, no trusted setup, compact chain.

03 · MimbleWimble

A blockchain where amounts are hidden, yet every transaction stays mathematically verifiable.

Proposed anonymously in 2016 by "Tom Elvis Jedusor"; formalised by Andrew Poelstra. Live in production as Grin and Beam. The next slides build it from four ideas.

04 · Idea 1 — hide the amount

Pedersen commitment.

A commitment is a locked box for a number. The amount v is locked behind a random blinding factor r. Hiding: r is uniform, so the box reveals nothing about v. Binding: you cannot later open the box to a different amount, because no one knows how the two generators G and H relate.

C = v·H + r·G
05 · Idea 2 — check the balance

Commitments add up.

Pedersen commitments are homomorphic — adding two boxes gives the box of the sum. So the network can confirm money in = money out + fee by adding the commitments. If it balances, every amount term cancels and only a blinding term (the excess) remains. No amount is ever revealed.

ΣC_in − ΣC_out − fee·H
= excess·G
06 · Idea 3 — prove no inflation

The kernel signature.

The leftover excess·G is published as a public key. The spender signs it with a Schnorr signature. A valid signature proves two things at once: the amounts balanced, and the spender owns the inputs. An unbalanced transaction would leave an amount term the spender cannot sign for.

verify: s·G == R + e·P
07 · Idea 4 — block the cheat

Range proofs.

The balance check accepts negative amounts — a spender could commit to −5 and +100, "balance" with +95, and mint 5 from nothing. A range proof proves each amount lies in [0, 2⁶⁴) without revealing it, closing the attack. This project uses the classical bit-commitment construction (pre-Bulletproofs).

08 · Why it is secure

Two generators, one hard problem.

  • secp256k1, the same curve as Bitcoin. G is the standard generator; H is a second generator.
  • H is "nothing up my sleeve". Derived from a public seed by try-and-increment, so nobody knows a scalar k with H = k·G — which is exactly what keeps commitments binding.
  • Everything rests on the discrete-log problem. Given Q = k·G, recovering k is infeasible. Hiding, binding, and signature unforgeability all follow.

10 · The proposed solution

Verifying privacy on Ethereum.

  • verifyKernelSignature — confirms the spender knows the kernel excess (ownership).
  • verifyBalance — confirms Σ inputs − Σ outputs − fee·H equals the kernel excess (no inflation).
  • secp256k1 from scratch — point add, double, scalar-mul, modular inverse, since the EVM has no curve precompile.
  • Range proofs stay off-chain — on-chain they would cost tens of millions of gas; verified in the Python prover instead.
11 · Inside the verifier

The no-inflation check, in code.

Sum the input points, subtract each output point, subtract the fee term, and compare the result to the published kernel excess. If the amounts did not balance, an amount term would remain and the equality would fail.

uint256 x = inX[0]; uint256 y = inY[0];
for (i=1; i<inX.length; i++)        // add inputs
  (x,y) = ecAdd(x,y, inX[i],inY[i]);
for (i=0; i<outX.length; i++)       // subtract outputs
  (x,y) = ecAdd(x,y, outX[i],(PP-outY[i])%PP);
if (fee != 0) {                       // subtract fee·H
  (fx,fy) = ecMul(HX,HY,(NN-fee)%NN);
  (x,y)   = ecAdd(x,y, fx,fy);
}
return x==kernelExcessX && y==kernelExcessY;

14 · Scope & future work

Known limitations.

  • Cut-through & kernel aggregation — not implemented; this is the CT primitive layer.
  • Bulletproofs — would replace the classical range proof and shrink it roughly ten-fold.
  • Gas optimization — affine coordinates kept for clarity; Jacobian + wNAF would cut on-chain cost.
  • No P2P layer — the focus is the cryptographic core, not networking.
